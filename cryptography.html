Cryptocurrency and Blockchain

How is Bitcoin and the Blockchain?

In July 1999 Nobel Prize-winning economist Milton Friedman said "The Internet would be one of the major forces in reducing the role of government. One thing that is missing that will soon be developed is a reliable e-cash, a method whereby on the Internet, you can transfer funds from A to B without, A knowing B; or B knowing A" (National Taxpayers Union Foundation, 1999). This prediction became a reality in 2009 with the release of Bitcoin (BTC) by the pseudonymous Satoshi Nakamoto (Ammous, 2020).

Video 1. Milton Fiedman Predicts the future of Cryptocurrencies.

Cryptocurrencies have the potential to change the way society transfers funds from one person to another (Mercer, 2019). Because cryptocurrencies, such as BTC, allow someone to transfer funds over a communications network without the need of Banks, Credit card companies or other trusted intermediary facilitating the transaction (Ammous, 2018). It also allows individuals to take control of their money and make payments pseudonymously (Murphy, Murphy and Seitzinger, 2013). Once a transaction is processed, it is impossible to reverse.    

However, the pseudonymous and unregulated nature of BTC opens the door to criminals, allowing them to cryptographically launder, evade taxes, make illegal sales and purchases such as drugs and firearms; and store the proceeds of crime. BTC is commonly used today by cybercriminals to blackmail people into paying them using cryptocurrencies. There are some notable examples of this. In 2017 the WannaCry ransomware attack encrypted a victim's data and gave them 72 hours to pay US$300 to a BTC address to decrypt it, or the cost will rise, and seven days before the files are lost forever (Ransomware WannaCry: All you need to know | Kaspersky, n.d.). Furthermore, an anonymous black-market site on the dark web, known as Silk Road founded in 2011 by Ross Ulbricht (Greenberg, 2020). 

Because a cryptocurrency is transferred from peer-to-peer directly, over a communications network, and without a trusted intermediary, a person could believe that this form of payment system is insecure, but, thanks to the blockchain this is not necessarily the case (Yuan and Wang, 2018). 

Figure 1. Image of the WannaCry interface (www.securehalo.com, n.d.)

A Cryptocurrency operates on top of the blockchain, which consists of tens of thousands of computers around the world called data miners. Data mining is the process of solving complex mathematical equations with the use of cryptography and regulates the production of new bitcoins. Miners verify transactions not formally confirmed by the network (FAQ - Bitcoin, 2020). When the validity of transactions are verified, they are appended to the block and become part of the blockchain; this becomes the new record of truth (Rosic, 2020). Once a miner solves the mathematical problem, it updates the ledger and sends it to all the other miners on the blockchain network.   

The blockchain is a decentralised, immutable network; meaning, the storage of data such as balances and transfers known as the ledger does not occur on a centralised computer or database (Rosic, 2020). 

Miners spend a vast amount of energy and processing power to verify the integrity of every transaction and currently costs around US$6,851 to make one bitcoin and could rise as high as US12,525 (Keoun, 2020). As of 2020 the reward for adding a block to the blockchain in 6.25 BTC for each added block. That is a staggering US$42,818.75 compared to US$44,043.87 when comparing it to the US$1,225.17 profit margin; it hardly seems worth it considering the setup costs (Marquit, 2020).

It is impossible to hack the blockchain because of its decentralised, immutable nature. Hacking it can only be successfully achieved by altering the ledger on fifty-one per cent of all miners on the blockchain network at precisely the same time. (What Is a 51% Attack? | Binance Academy, n.d.).    

Hacking the blockchain should not be confused with hacking a BTC wallet. A BTC wallet can be lost, stolen or destroyed and is the owner's responsibility to protect and backup their wallets. A wallet, not backed up, will become unrecoverable, and in both cases, all their funds will also be lost and unrecoverable but remain dormant on the Blockchain (FAQ - Bitcoin, 2020). There is an example of this occurring in 2011 the deletion of Bitomat's wallet.dat file while increasing their virtual machines ram (Dotson, 2020); that failure caused a loss of 17000 BTC at a value of US$13 each and at the time a total value of US$221,000. Currently, the BTC is valued at US$7047.02 and equates to a total worth of US$120,000,000 (Dotson, 2020). 

Cryptocurrencies will have both positive and negative impacts on society because cybersecurity is as much about the user's ability to maintain a safe, secure system, and by following good security practices (Malan, 2017). These impacts will be primarily dependent on the user to protect their Cryptographic assets. The user should weigh up the pros and cons before choosing a cryptocurrency wallet and determine if cryptocurrencies are appropriate for their individual needs. 

The individual must then take the appropriate measures to maintain the integrity of their wallet and keep it secure because they will most certainly feel the negative impacts of their failure to keep those assets secure (Carter, 2017). Some of these measures should most certainly involve multiple wallet backups, use offline storage to secure the files on physical encrypted USB, hard drives, and only use encrypted online storage methods (Securing your wallet - Bitcoin, 2020)(IMPELLI, 2020).

There are positives with cryptocurrencies. In the year leading up to 2018, BTC owners enjoyed a growth rate near one-thousand-eight-hundred per cent (Higgins, 2020). Although BTC fell dramatically in the following year to 2019 (Bambrough, 2018), today, BTC is worth US$7042.02 This amount of money is extensive when compared to the United States dollar, but that also depends how and when a BTC was acquired, and because of the cost of making one BTC is close to its current value, mining could soon become a financial liability.

Figure 2. Graph displaying the sudden rise and fall of bitcoins value (www.bitcoin.org, n.d.).

It is hard to determine what the personal effects of cryptocurrencies or Blockchain technology will be. However, after extensive research, it has become clear that the future of Blockchain technology is bright. Numerous corporations are such as Banks, governments (Government office for Science, United Kingdom., 2016) and education institutions are investigating the notion that they can implement this technology (Raths, 2020). Banks and governments will most definitely want to govern the use of the blockchain. Education institutions are researching the possibility that an individuals transcript can be decentralised and accessed and appended to by all institutions and organisations. 

I want more control of my money, and cryptocurrencies, have the potential to assist in achieving this financial independence a reality. Thus, having a positive impact when considered from that point of view. However, I question what affects this will have on my ability to apply for credit, which makes this transition either out of reach for the majority of the global population including myself who rely on credit cards and mortgages to build financial and security stability. The evidence suggests that there are rewards for investing in cryptocurrencies. Like gambling, you win some; you lose some. At this stage in the evolution of BTC, notably. I would not take the gamble buying into the market, nor would I invest in data mining. The risks are vast, costs are tremendous, and the negative impacts would be financially catastrophic.
